Simulation of long-term spatio-temporal environmental dynamics using a unified benchmark of neighbor augmenting, LSTM and graph attention models

Kaveh Karimadini (Macquarie University) · Sharareh Pourebrahim (Sunway University)*

NA-LSTM best · R² ≈ 0.95 Klang River Basin · 2000–2024 CC BY-NC-ND 4.0 Open source · MIT
Read the paper (DOI) Code repository Explore results interactively

Abstract

Rapid environmental change has increased the need for predicting long-term geospatial dynamics reliably. However, accurately modeling spatio-temporal geospatial dynamics remains challenging because of nonlinearities, complex spatial dependency, and external driving factors. This paper proposes a comprehensive benchmarking framework for comparing neighborhood-based, graph-based, and attention-based spatiotemporal deep learning models under the same preprocessing, training, and testing procedure.

Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models with and without auxiliary variables are compared with hybrid Graph Attention Network–LSTM (GAT–LSTM) models and fully attention-based GAT–Temporal Attention models, with and without a feed-forward (MLP/FFN) block. All models are trained using a unified preprocessing and evaluation pipeline on annual satellite data in NetCDF from 2000 to 2023, with 2024 reserved as a fully unseen test dataset. Global pixel-wise measures (R², RMSE, MAE, MAPE, correlation) evaluate model performance based on alignment of predicted and reference vectors.

Key findings: LSTM–CA with auxiliary inputs (3×3 neighborhood) achieves the best and most stable performance (R² ≈ 0.95), highlighting the importance of integrated Cellular Automata structure and auxiliary driving factors. GAT–Temporal Attention with an MLP block ranks second. Removing the MLP or using hybrid LSTM–GAT configurations leads to unstable or degraded performance. Vegetation- and water-related indices are more predictable. Strong temporal modeling combined with auxiliary information matters more than spatial attention complexity.

Keywords: Spatio-temporal forecasting; Geospatial time series; LSTM; Graph Attention Network (GAT); Environmental monitoring.

Novelty: This work does not introduce a new neural network family — it provides a comparative engineering experiment assessing when neighborhood-based temporal models can be superior to graph-attention models in geospatial long-range prediction.

1. Introduction

Spatio-temporal prediction plays a central role in transportation systems, air pollution and weather monitoring, land-use planning, agricultural productivity assessment, smart city development, environmental management, and public health. Reliable forecasting supports proactive planning, risk mitigation, and efficient resource allocation. However, learning the joint evolution of data across space and time remains challenging due to complex, nonlinear, and non-stationary processes and the growing volume of sensor and satellite observations.

1.2 Advances and Limitations in Recent Spatio-Temporal Models

Since around 2021, deep learning has become dominant in spatio-temporal forecasting. Hybrid GNN + RNN/LSTM architectures gained popularity in traffic and mobility. Transformer-based models (e.g., Informer) enabled long-range temporal dependencies. Large-scale models like FourCastNet and GraphCast demonstrated remarkable potential for weather forecasting but highlight growing computational demands.

GAT is anisotropic — it learns edge-specific attention weights — whereas GCN is isotropic and limits modeling of asymmetric relationships. Emerging trends include multi-granularity fusion (MGSFformer), missing-data resilience (GinAR+), and non-stationary dynamics (MetaGNSDformer), all emphasizing the need for benchmarking tools that test accuracy, robustness, and generalization.

1.3 Hybrid Architectures: Progress and Limitations

Hybrid architectures integrate signal processing, graph learning, and recurrent/attention temporal modeling. VMD–GAT–BiLSTM achieved substantial MAE reductions in air quality forecasting. In LUCC modeling, LSTM–CA with class weighting outperformed Markov-based methods. However, spatio-temporal modeling faces model-form and parametric uncertainties, and most studies introduce new architectures rather than systematically comparing fundamentally different paradigms under a common framework — particularly for long-term generalization to a fully unseen future time step.

1.4 Research Gap and Motivation

Two main gaps motivate this work:

  1. Lack of engineering-level comparative studies evaluating neighborhood-augmented LSTM against graph-attention models under unified preprocessing, training, and testing.
  2. Most studies emphasize short-term accuracy while long-term generalization to an entirely unseen future year remains underexplored — critical for forward-looking planning.

Three strategies are compared under controlled conditions: NA-LSTM, GAT–LSTM, and GAT–Temporal Attention.

1.5 Objective

The main objective is a controlled benchmarking framework comparing neighborhood-augmented LSTM and graph-attention architectures for one-year-ahead prediction of multi-layer geospatial states (2000–2023 train, 2023→2024 test). Three complementary approaches are evaluated:

1. NA-LSTM3×3 neighborhood augmentation + LSTM temporal transition rules.
2. GAT–LSTMGraph attention for spatial dependencies + LSTM temporal modeling.
3. GAT–Temporal AttentionGAT spatial encoding + Transformer-style self-attention (± MLP).

2. Materials and Methods

The framework comprises: (i) harmonized spatio-temporal preprocessing, (ii) spatial feature construction via CA neighborhood encoding or graph attention, (iii) temporal modeling via LSTM or multi-head self-attention, (iv) supervised one-step-ahead training with Huber loss, and (v) post-processing with vectorized multi-band evaluation.

2.1 Study Area

The Klang River Basin in western Peninsular Malaysia covers approximately 127,326 hectares, including Kuala Lumpur and large areas of Selangor. As Malaysia's main administrative, commercial, and financial region, the basin is highly urbanized with rapid land-use change, altered hydrological processes, and environmental pressure — making it a suitable testbed for long-term spatio-temporal forecasting.

2.2 Data Sources and Structure

Primary targetAnnual multi-band Landsat imagery (5 bands), 2000–2024.
Dynamic auxiliaryAnnually varying NDVI aligned to the Landsat grid.
Static auxiliaryDEM, slope, distance-to-road/river rasters (time-invariant).
Split2000–2023 development; 2024 exclusively unseen test.

2.3 Data Preprocessing

All inputs resampled to a common longitude–latitude grid (H × W = 1825 × 1867), organized into annual steps. Border cells handled for complete 3×3 neighborhoods. Missing/invalid pixels zero-filled prior to normalization. Static variables screened with Cramer's V in IDRISI TerrSet (retain V ≥ 0.40; exclude V < 0.15). All models share identical prepared inputs — performance differences arise from architecture, not data inconsistencies.

2.4 Forecasting Setup and Experimental Design

One-step-ahead pairing: Xt → Yt+1, samples from 2000→2001 through 2022→2023 for training; 2023→2024 exclusively for testing.

2.5 Feature Engineering

2.5.1 Spatial Neighborhood Encoding

For cell (i, j), the 3×3 neighborhood set:

Ni,j = {(i+Δi, j+Δj) | Δi, Δj ∈ {−1, 0, 1}} (1)

Spatially expanded feature vector per depth layer k:

xt(k)(i,j) = [Xt(k)(p,q)](p,q)∈Ni,j (2)

Stacking all 5 depth layers yields xt(i,j) ∈ ℝ5×9.

2.5.2 Auxiliary Feature Integration

x̃t(i,j) = [xt(i,j), at(d)(i,j), a(s)(i,j)] (3)

2.5.3–2.5.4 Temporal Pairing & Normalization

x̃t → yt+1,   t ∈ [2000, 2022] for training (4)
xnorm(c) = (x(c) − min(x(c))) / (max(x(c)) − min(x(c))) (5)

2.6 LSTM-Based Forecasting Model

Standard LSTM gates (equations 6–11): input, forget, cell, output gates with sigmoid/tanh activations.

ŷt+1 = σ(Wo(ReLU(Whht + bh)) + bo) (12)

Huber loss (equation 13) balances MSE-like behavior with outlier robustness. Predictions denormalized via inverse min–max (equation 15).

2.8 Graph-Based Spatio-Temporal Models

Raster grids converted to graphs G = (V, E): each pixel is a node (|V| = H×W), edges via 4-connectivity. Sparse COO edge indices; batch size 1 for memory efficiency.

4. Case Study

4.1 One-Year-Ahead Forecasting (2023 → 2024)

All models evaluated on the held-out 2023→2024 transition. NA-LSTM with auxiliary inputs shows the tightest clustering around the 1:1 line in scatter plots. LSTM with auxiliaries achieved highest overall accuracy (average R² ≈ 0.95). GAT–Temporal Attention with MLP ranked second (R² ≈ 0.87). Models without MLP and LSTM–GAT hybrids showed substantially weaker performance (R² ≈ 0.34 or lower for several indices).

4.2 Model-Level Performance Comparison

NA-LSTM with auxiliaries consistently outperformed all others on RMSE, MAE, correlation, and R². GAT–TA with MLP was competitive on vegetation/water indices but inferior overall. Negative R² values indicate performance worse than a mean baseline — a sign of weak generalization, not calculation error.

4.3 Temporal Robustness (Rolling-Origin CV)

NA-LSTM with aux achieved highest average R² (0.913) despite far fewer parameters. GAT–TA with S-GAT=2 achieved second-highest average (0.880), indicating better multi-year robustness than S-GAT=1 despite lower 2024 holdout performance. Auxiliary inputs improved NA-LSTM from 0.740→0.913 and GAT–TA from 0.814→0.856 average R².

4.5 Ablation and Architectural Comparisons

Grid: 1825×1867 nodes, ~4HW edges. NA-LSTM: 10,725–16,485 params vs GAT–TA: 54,981–59,269. Experiments on CPU (35–40 GB RAM) due to full-raster memory requirements.

4.7 Uncertainty and Robustness Analysis

All models degraded under two-step input framing. NA-LSTM without aux showed largest drop (0.786→0.217). Under Gaussian noise, NA-LSTM+aux maintained highest R² at mild/moderate noise (0.910 at σ=0.01, 0.879 at σ=0.03) but converged with GAT models at σ=0.05. GAT–TA showed best relative noise robustness at high intensity.

4.8 Metric Behavior and Outliers

MAPE highly sensitive near zero-valued indices. Negative R² retained as diagnostic of model instability. Interpret MAPE and R² jointly with MAE, RMSE, and correlation.

5. Discussion

Better NA-LSTM performance with auxiliaries confirms that long-term geospatial dynamics are controlled by both multi-year temporal relationships and exogenous inputs. Spectral history alone is insufficient; static and dynamic covariates provide complementary information for generalization to unseen future conditions.

The 3×3 neighborhood inductive bias aligns with local environmental dynamics where adjacent pixels share similar features. Unlike GAT, which must learn spatial weights from limited annual samples, NA-LSTM encodes local structure directly. GAT–TA with MLP outperforms alternatives but modest gains over LSTM+aux suggest temporal dynamics and auxiliary drivers dominate at this forecasting horizon. Deeper GAT may smooth heterogeneous land-cover contrast.

The large gap between temporal attention with and without MLP demonstrates that nonlinear feature transformation is essential — consistent with standard Transformer designs where the FFN is core to representation learning.

LSTM–GAT instability may arise from sequential interaction of two complex blocks — spatial encoding errors propagate into temporal learning. Simpler, well-conditioned architectures like NA-LSTM+aux exhibit more stable long-term extrapolation.

EVI and BSI show reduced predictability across models. Index-wise evaluation is essential. MAPE unreliable near zero. Future work should include spatial-zone validation by land-use class, elevation, and ecological subregion.

Only ~23 annual transitions available; spatial samples are autocorrelated. Regularization (dropout, Huber loss, held-out year, rolling CV) mitigates but high-capacity models remain at risk. Good holdout performance should be interpreted cautiously.

Conclusion

This study proposed a unified benchmarking framework for one-step-ahead long-term spatio-temporal geospatial forecasting. Under harmonized preprocessing, consistent train–test splitting, and vectorized pixel-wise evaluation, NA-LSTM with auxiliary inputs achieved the best and most stable performance (R² ≈ 0.95).

Main takeaways:

  • Robust temporal modeling + external driving factors > spatial attention complexity.
  • S-GAT=1 better on 2024 holdout; S-GAT=2 more robust across rolling years.
  • MLP/FFN essential for temporal attention architectures.
  • Vegetation/water indices (NDVI, NDWI, SAVI) more predictable than EVI, BSI.
  • MAPE unreliable near zero — use MAE, RMSE, correlation, R² jointly.

Future work: multi-step forecasting, uncertainty quantification (ensembles, MC dropout), multi-region transferability, self-supervised Earth observation models, and explainable AI (attention maps, feature attribution).

Reproduce

Place annual NetCDF stacks under data/ (landsat20*.nc, optional ndvi20*.nc, plus dem.nc, slope.nc, road_dis.nc).

pip install -r requirements.txt

# Best configuration (NA-LSTM + auxiliaries)
python main.py --model nalstm --use-ndvi --use-static-features --no-downsample

# GAT–Temporal Attention with MLP
python main.py --model gattemporalattention --use-feed-forward --use-ndvi --use-static-features

# Noise robustness analysis
python uncertainty.py --model gattemporalattention \
  --checkpoint-path checkpoints/your_model.pth \
  --perturbation-mode noise --use-feed-forward
